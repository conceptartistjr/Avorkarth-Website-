Attributes

Attributes are the character's ability to affect and be affected by the world, the statistics of the character's capabilities which can be used against the world's obstacles, often against the player's will. Such things are how quickly the character moves or how many wounds they can take before they are downed.

Creature Size

Size represents the approximation of how much space a creature takes upon the field, be it through height or width, split into the game tiles. Each tile cannot be shared by multiple creatures unless they are the Small scales, or a special rule allows them to be in the same space as another.
Size's benefits are two-fold. Larger creatures are easier to hit but will deal more damage in return.

Front/Flank/Rear
Most creatures can only face a single direction at a time. This is their front, which usually gives bonuses to defense. If they are attacked from the sides, that would be the flank and at worse, they are attacked from the back. For creatures with a tail, attacking any part of this is counted as attacking their back.

Vision

Vision, or Sight, is applied mostly when a character is in a darkened area, such as a cave or during the night. Typical human vision in daylight can go for miles on end, but can only see about 5 tiles ahead of them while under a dark moonlit night. Inhuman characters may have better or worse eyesights, which are shown below.

Movement

Movement is how quickly a character moves through the world. The actual movement attribute is generally unused outside of dangerous situations or combat. While combat is active, movement is shown as 1 for each tile, with diagonals counting as 1 and a half. A character can move through these tiles at the rate of their chosen movement attribute, as long as they are not occupied.

Maneuverability
This determines the ease of travel for the character upon different terrains.
- Perfect : The character may move freely.
- Good : The character cannot move in diagonals, but can turn in the same move action.
- Rough : The character may only move in a straight line and must take a second move action to turn.

Agile Movement
While movement is mostly determined by racial bases, it can be increased or decreased by the character's Agility. Each Agility higher or lower than the racial base average increases their highest movement by 1. Each 2 Agility increases the rest by 1.

Sprinting
A character may use two movement actions in a turn to sprint. If so used, they may add +1 to their total movement, +1 more per 5 in their Run speed.

Swimming/Climbing
Though swimming and climbing are skills, they technically are movement sub-sets as well. Characters who attempt to swim through deep waters or climb steep mountain-sides may do so only if they pass the skill check.

Flying
Characters that have a flying movement speed may use it instead of moving regularily. Their distance from the ground (moving "up") counts as movement as well and must be noted. Flight can only be used if the character is in their Light or lower lifting capacity.

Falling
Falling is commonplace in areas of danger. Cliffs, traps, ravines and ledges are all obstacles where falling is possible. Falling damage begins once the drop is 2 tiles or greater (+1 tile per Size difference greater than medium). For each tile above the fall damage distance, the character takes 2 lethal damage, reduced by the character's tumble skill roll.

Breathing Underwater/Holding a breath
If the character is not amphibious, they may hold their breath underwater a number of rounds equal to 5 + their constitution score. Afterwards, they take 1 lethal damage per round.



Lifting Capacity

Upon their journeys and adventures, characters are often burdened with gear, treasures and items of various usefulness. Unfortunately, characters can only carry so much, determined in part by their Strength Ability Score and in part by their Size. Strength determines the character's maximum weight allowance, including gear being worn. Lifting Capacity determines the total weight of all objects upon the character's person that they may carry before becoming burdened. Different penalties or bonuses are given based upon their current weight and strength.

Multiple Values (Optional)

The lifting capacity of a character may be choosen from any lifting capacity between the ability below up to their maximum in the chart. As an example, a character whose strength tier is 6 has a light carrying capacity from 20 to 40. Choose that which makes the most sense to the character.

Lifting Capacity Penalties

When reaching a threshold of the character's lifting capacity, the character unfortunately takes on penalties to their movement as well as movement based attributes.

Light load or lower : No Penalties
Light to Medium load : -1 to Dodge value, Movement and Agility based skills.
Medium to Heavy load : Halve Dodge, Movement and Agility skill values or -2 (worse one).
Heavy Load to Maximum : Cannot Dodge, 1 tile movement, can only use non-movement based skills and only one action per turn.
Greater than Maximum Load : Cannot move or take any actions.

Carrying Capacity

No matter a character's strength, their carrying amount is unfortunately still limited by their size. A character may only carry so many items of certain sizes, as noted below. Each item size is equivalent to a medium sized creature and increases along with. So a standard sized sword for a medium creature is a large sword for a small creature.

Note as well that clothing or armour does NOT count towards this capacity, but worn items such as swords and backpacks do.

Languages

All civilized cultures throughout the realm of creation have created specialized forms of communication. These languages are often complex vocalizations unique to their species, although that doesn't mean that they aren't learnable. The complexity of a language is shown by a points score. Depending on how many points within a language the character has, they may either understand, speak or read and write the said language.

Additional Languistic Points

Intelligence : At intelligence 5, a character gains 1 additional language point. For every point of intelligence above, the character may gain up to 1 additional language point per 10 years of age, to a maximum of 3 times their intelligence tier.
Example : A character with an intelligence of 6 would have 2 language point at the age of 20+ and 3 at the age of 30+, up to a maximum of 18. If their intelligence is 7, they would get 3 languge points at the age of 20+ and 5 at the age of 30+, to a maximum of 21.

Backstory : Languages learned by the character MUST match their backstory. If a highly intelligent character would include draconic in their language, they must have a method of learning said language in their backstory. Alternatively, if a character is not of high enough intelligence but the character's backstory truly demands that they know a certain language outside of their usual, this can be brought up to the GM and added as well if it fits fine.

Health (Constitution)

The total health a character has represents the amount of wounds that the character can take before either falling unconscious or dying. There are two ways of wounding a character, one being Non-Lethal attacks (Hit points) and the other being Lethal attacks (Health points).

HEALTH

Total WoundsConstitution + Other bonuses

Non-lethal Damage : Whenever a character takes enough wounds from a non-lethal amount to surpass their health total, they fall into the unconcious state.
If the character has equal non-lethal wound points to their health, they fall into the unconscious or the bleeding out states.
If the character has wound points equal to their Health Points, they die.

Unconscious State : While in such a state, the character cannot move or take any actions and is essentially removed from the game until after the combat ends, where they are then awake once more at 1 Hit point. Any non-lethal damage taken after the character falls unconscious is converted to lethal damage.

Bleeding Out State : If a character falls unconscious from lethal damage, they reach the bleeding out state instead. They begin taking 1 lethal damage per round.

Lethal and Non-Lethal interaction
Should a character take Lethal damage, it deals both lethal and non-lethal damage for equal amounts. However, if a character takes non-lethal damage, it only affects the non-lethal hit points.

Regaining Hit Points
Hit points are regained by simply resting, returning at 1 + 1 per positive Constitution ability score per 8 hours rest. Hit points cannot ever go above the current health points of the character.

Regaining Health Points (Heal Counter)
Health points cannot be regained by simply resting, and the wounds must be taken care of with the heal skill or magic. Every wound point requires 10 heal counters.

Attack Values (Vs. Dodge Values)

The attack value attribute is used in combat to determine the effectiveness of a character ability with weaponry, be it sword, bow, fist, claw or maw. Characters may increase their melee attack by two styles, either dextrous precision or agile speed. Ranged attacks are only increased by the precision of the character. The attack values are compared against the Dodge defense and any attack value below 1 is counted as a 1.




MELEE ATTACK VALUE

Agility OR Dexterity Score + Weapon Bonus + Weapon Proficiency

RANGED ATTACK VALUE

Dexterity Score + Weapon Bonus + Weapon Proficiency

Striking (Vs. Armour Values)

Any attack which successfully hits their target is then re-rolled as a strike, to see if it goes through the enemies' hide or armour.

STRIKING POWER

Weapon Might + Strength Score
*Some items, such as crossbows, can't be increased by strength.

Unarmed : Creatures without weapons or natural attacks have a base non-lethal crushing weapon of 0 might and 1 non-lethal damage that doesn't count as a natural weapon. It is considered Unskilled for proficiency.

Wounding

Weapon Damage : All weapons also have a damage number, which shows how many wounds the character's target is dealt upon a successful strike, increased by +1 per strength category above "Standard". (See Ability Scores)

DAMAGE DEALT

Weapon Damage + Strength Category*
*Only + 1 per strength category above standard

Size Differences

Whenever a creature attacks something of greater or lesser size than themselves, they gain bonuses or take penalties. Larger creatures deal more damage to smaller ones, while smaller creatures deal less damage to those greater than themselves, but with an easier time to hit.

SIZE BONUSES/PENALTIES*

Attacking any Size Greater+1 attack value per size difference, -1 damage dealt per size difference.
Attacking any Size Smaller-1 attack value per size difference, +1 damage dealt, doubling per size difference


Weapon Mastery

While wielding weaponry, characters may require to be proficient with them or else take a penalty. When creating a character, the character may choose which weapons they are skilled with. This is covered more in the Mastery section of the rulebook.

Base Proficinecies: If a character has a proficiency of practiced or greater with a weapon of a certain type, all other weapons of similar type are considered Unskilled instead of Untrained. For example a practiced scimitar would allow untrained for all other one-handed swords.

Natural Weapons : Natural weapons are considered at the Practiced level, although may also be increased.

Dodge

Dodging is the primary method of defense, showing the character's ability to weave through combat and evade blows, arrows or spells.

DODGE

Agility Score + Instinct Score - 5 + Armour Penalty

Directional Dodge

While in combat, the character's placing matters. As discussed in the previous placing section, a character has a front, flank and back. This affects dodge rather harshly.

DIRECTIONAL DODGE

FrontFront attacks retain full dodge.
FlankFlank attacks reduce dodge by 1 category.
BackBack attacks reduce dodge value by 3 categories.

Blocking/Parrying

The secondary part of defense, should an attacking partially hit, is to block a part of the incoming damage. Parrying is influenced by one's arm strength to be able to hold back a blow as well as one's proficiency with weapons. Parrying increases one's armour amount before calculating the armour defense.

Blocking : If a character has a shield, they may instead block the attack.

PARRY BONUS

Strength Score + Weapon Mastery

BLOCK BONUS

Strength Score + Shield Guarded Value


Armour

Armour is the tertiary and final method of defense, from armoured plating to thick hides, it is used for any attack that isn't dodged or parried. It is used to counter the attacker's strike and to possibly negate incoming damage.

Armour Types

Full Armour
Armours are traditionally really good at defending head on, making most items or bonuses state that they give a Full Armour bonus. This bonus applies to any attack that is made to the character, as long as they are aware they are in combat. Therefore, attacks against targets that aren't able to respond ignore both Full and Guarded Armour.

Guarded Armour
Most armours, alongside shields, better protect attacks that are directed towards the front or flanks of the character but less so to attacks made from the back. Guarded armour values are only applied to attacks made from the front and flank.

Natural Armour
Some creatures have thick hides or plating. These are natural to the creature and as such cannot be countered by positioning unless specifically stated. Most skin has a value of 0.

GUARDED ARMOUR

Guarded Armour + Natural Armour

FULL ARMOUR

Full Armour + Natural Armour

NATURAL ARMOUR

Natural Armour

Armour Penalty

Armour Penalty : Armour within the world will protect one's skin, but will hinder one's movement. Most armours come with an Armour Penalty, which reduces two aspects. The first is a character's ability to dodge, the other in a character's ability to move freely within the world with many of the physical skills. These are noted in the dodge section and skills chapter respectively.



Resistance

Resistances function exactly like armour, although against any effects other than physical damage. These include spells, curses or environmental challenges such as lava or acid.

Negative Resistances
Should a creature have a negative score in their resistances, they take reduced healing equal to the negative of the resistance from any spells of that realm, and similarily take additional damage equal to the negative if ever struck by that sphere of damage.
For example : Undead would have negative Holy resistances, so they'd take reduced healing and increased damage from holy spells and effects.

Initiative

Initiative determines how quickly the character reacts to combat and how swiftly they enact their actions. It is used in combat to determine who goes first, second and so on.

INITIATIVE

Agility Score + Instinct Score OR Wisdom Score

Fright/Charm (Will)

A character's Will is ever important, giving them the courage to stand up against their foes and the heart to resist dangerous temptations. However, even the most hardened veteran may fall to the terrors or charms of battle and spells. Whenever an effect says that the character gains a charm or fear point, they must refer to this chart to see if any effect is yet placed on the character.

Should a character be suffering a charm and fear effect at the same time, they must suffer the effect of whichever is worse at the moment.

Regaining Sanity : Characters lose 1 fear and charm point per night's rest.

Reputation (Optional)

Reputation is a passive attribute meant as a method for the Game Master to track how certain creatures or other non-playable characters would react to the players. Reputation is usually linked to factions of every race, although if a Game Master wishes, or for simpler short campaigns, he can simply reduce it to the base per race basis. Of course, there are always exceptions to every race. Those certain few who are more open to diplomacy or easier to anger.

Reputation is split into two categories. The first is aptly named reputation, the second is fame. These work hand in hand to create a general picture of how the character is perceived at first notice.

Reputation vs Fame

Reputation
Reputation is simply put as how well your character is valued. This doesn't affect how well known your character is, it only tells how others of that race or faction will react upon seeing your character. Note that reputation can be split from the specific to the overall, and is where fame helps out.
E.g. : An harpy character can be well liked by a human village they helped save from a maurauding orc band, but they will still be quickly hunted or chased by humans of other areas if they aren't known for their heroic act.

Fame
Fame shows how well a character is known upon the continent of Avorkarth, be they famous or infamous. The greater the fame, the more likely the character is to be recognized, even if it's only for the city guards to quickly try to lock them up.

Reputation Lists

Reputation lists are where the player may note down and track their reputation. It is the Game Master's duty to create a Reputation sheet for the campaign they made. If they pick a pre-existing campaign, a reputation sheet would be already created for them, including all required factions that the players would meet as they go through the story. If one is to create a reputation sheet, keep in mind that each race (To be implemented...) has pre-existing racial reputations in their lore section.
E.g. : Humans would have a friendly reputation with the Narei elves, as they've often allied against darker causes.



Specific Reputation vs. Racial Reputation

While individuals can be influenced and learn to trust or even admire those not of their own race, the overall racial reputations never change. This is due to it being more of a guideline, a reasonable guess of how members of that species will do in response of meeting the character.
Compare this with the character's reputation with a guild or a particular caste, where the members would be told of the character, including their appearance and past actions. This would make them either more reasonable or aggressive, depending on the reputation and fame amongst them.

Reputation Chart

Together, fame and reputation mark your character upon the Reputation Chart, giving a title of sorts for each faction that the player knows of, with neutral in the middle of it all. Reputation dictates the direction of the chart, and Fame dictates the distance from the neutral point.
